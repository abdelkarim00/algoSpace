Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica

Book I: Axioms (Laws of Motion)

By Sir Isaac Newton (1687)

Following his Definitions, Newton presents three fundamental Axioms that became the foundation of classical mechanics. These Laws of Motion describe the relationship between a body and the forces acting upon it, and its motion in response to those forces.

Law I: The Law of Inertia

"Corpus omne perseverare in statu suo quiescendi vel movendi uniformiter in directum, nisi quatenus a viribus impressis cogitur statum illum mutare."

"Every body perseveres in its state of rest, or of uniform motion in a right line, unless it is compelled to change that state by forces impressed thereon."

Modern Interpretation

An object will remain at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.

∑F = 0 ⇒ Δv = 0

Example: A book resting on a table remains at rest until you lift it (applied force). A hockey puck sliding on ice continues moving until friction (external force) stops it.

Key implications:

  • Objects have a natural tendency to maintain their state of motion
  • Changes in motion require explanation (forces)
  • Contradicted Aristotle's view that motion required continuous force
Law II: The Law of Acceleration

"Mutationem motus proportionalem esse vi motrici impressæ, & fieri secundum lineam rectam qua vis illa imprimitur."

"The alteration of motion is ever proportional to the motive force impressed; and is made in the direction of the right line in which that force is impressed."

Modern Interpretation

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

F = ma
a = F/m

Example: Pushing a shopping cart with twice the force produces twice the acceleration (if mass stays the same). Two identical carts loaded together (double mass) need twice the force for the same acceleration.

Key points:

  • Force and acceleration are vectors (have direction)
  • Net force means the vector sum of all forces
  • Original formulation used momentum (p=mv): F = dp/dt
Law III: The Law of Action-Reaction

"Actioni contrariam semper & æqualem esse reactionem: sive corporum duorum actiones in se mutuo semper esse æquales & in partes contrarias dirigi."

"To every action there is always opposed an equal reaction: or the mutual actions of two bodies upon each other are always equal, and directed to contrary parts."

Modern Interpretation

When one body exerts a force on a second body, the second body simultaneously exerts a force equal in magnitude and opposite in direction on the first body.

FAB = -FBA

Example: When you jump, your legs push against the ground (action), and the ground pushes back with equal force (reaction), propelling you upward. Rockets work by expelling gas downward, which pushes the rocket upward.

Important notes:

  • Forces always come in pairs
  • Action and reaction act on different objects
  • Forces are of the same type (both gravitational, both contact, etc.)

Corollaries

Newton follows the Laws with six Corollaries that extend their application:

Corollary I

A body acted on by two forces simultaneously will describe the diagonal of a parallelogram in the same time as it would describe the sides by those forces separately.

Modern: Forces add vectorially (parallelogram law).

Corollary II

Explains how to resolve forces into components using the parallelogram law.

Corollary III

The quantity of motion (momentum) in a closed system is conserved in all directions.

∑pinitial = ∑pfinal

Corollary IV

The common center of gravity of multiple bodies does not change its state of motion unless acted upon by external forces.

Corollary V

In a system where bodies move among themselves, motions are the same whether the system is at rest or moving uniformly forward.

Modern: Galilean relativity - laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames.

Corollary VI

If bodies are moving in any way among themselves and are equally accelerated in parallel directions, they continue to move among themselves in the same way as if they were not accelerated.
