Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica

Book I: The Motion of Bodies - Definitions

By Sir Isaac Newton (1687)

Newton begins his Principia with eight definitions that establish the conceptual framework for his laws of motion and universal gravitation. These definitions precisely define the fundamental quantities used throughout the work.

Definition I: Quantity of Matter (Mass)

"Quantitas materiæ est mensura ejusdem orta ex illius densitate et magnitudine conjunctim."

"The quantity of matter is the measure of the same, arising from its density and bulk conjointly."

Modern Interpretation

Mass (m) is an intrinsic property of matter that measures its resistance to acceleration (inertial mass) and its gravitational attraction (gravitational mass). Newton's definition combines density (ρ) and volume (V):

m = ρ × V

In modern physics, mass is considered one of the fundamental properties of matter, measured in kilograms (kg) in the SI system.

Definition II: Quantity of Motion (Momentum)

"Quantitas motus est mensura ejusdem orta ex velocitate et quantitate materiæ conjunctim."

"The quantity of motion is the measure of the same, arising from the velocity and quantity of matter conjointly."

Modern Interpretation

Momentum (p) is the product of an object's mass and its velocity:

p = m × v

Momentum is a vector quantity (having both magnitude and direction) that is conserved in isolated systems. This conservation principle is fundamental to analyzing collisions and other interactions.

Definition III: Inertia

"Materiæ vis insita est potentia resistendi..."

"The innate force of matter is a power of resisting..."

Modern Interpretation

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes to its state of motion. This is expressed in Newton's First Law (the Law of Inertia). The measure of an object's inertia is its mass - more massive objects have greater inertia.

Key aspects:

  • Objects at rest remain at rest unless acted upon
  • Objects in motion continue at constant velocity unless acted upon
  • Inertia is not a force, but a property of matter

Definition IV: Impressed Force

"Vis impressa est actio in corpus exercita..."

"An impressed force is an action exerted upon a body..."

Modern Interpretation

Force is any interaction that changes the motion of an object. In modern terms:

F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration)

Forces can be:

  • Contact forces (pushes, pulls)
  • Field forces (gravity, electromagnetism)
  • Measured in Newtons (N) in the SI system (1 N = 1 kg·m/s²)

Definition V: Centripetal Force

"Vis centripeta est qua corpora versus punctum aliquod..."

"A centripetal force is that by which bodies are drawn or impelled toward a point..."

Modern Interpretation

Centripetal force is any force that causes an object to follow a curved path, always directed toward the center of curvature:

F = mv²/r

Where:

  • m = mass of the object
  • v = velocity
  • r = radius of the circular path

Examples include gravity (planetary orbits) and tension (swinging a ball on a string).
