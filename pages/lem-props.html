Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica

Book I: Lemmas & Propositions

By Sir Isaac Newton (1687)

Following the Definitions and Laws of Motion, Newton presents a series of mathematical Lemmas (preliminary results) and Propositions that develop the theoretical framework for his physics. These establish the foundations of calculus and their application to motion under centripetal forces.

Section I: Method of First and Last Ratios

Newton's Lemmas I-XI develop his "method of fluxions" (calculus) through geometric arguments about limits.

Lemma I: Equal Areas in Equal Times

"Quantitates, ut & quantitatum rationes, quæ ad æqualitatem dato tempore constanter tendunt & eo pacto propius ad invicem accedere possunt quam pro data quavis differentia, fiunt ultimo æquales."

"Quantities, and the ratios of quantities, which in any finite time converge continually to equality, and before the end of that time approach nearer to each other than by any given difference, become ultimately equal."

Modern Interpretation

This is Newton's geometric formulation of the limit concept, stating that if two quantities become arbitrarily close over time, they approach the same limit.

lim (xₙ - yₙ) = 0 ⇒ lim xₙ = lim yₙ

This lemma underpins Newton's development of calculus throughout the Principia.

Newton's Proof: Uses geometric argument about areas under curves approaching equality as intervals diminish.

Lemma III: Ultimate Ratios

"The ultimate ratios which vanishing quantities bear to one another are actually ratios of the quantities before they vanish."

Modern Interpretation

This establishes the derivative as the limit of ratios:

lim (Δy/Δx) = dy/dx as Δx→0

Key applications:

  • Justifies finding tangents as limits of secant lines
  • Provides foundation for differential calculus
  • Used throughout Principia for orbital mechanics

Section II: Centripetal Forces

Propositions I-XX develop the theory of motion under centripetal forces.

Proposition I: Area Law

"Areas, quas corpora in gyros acta radiis ad centrum virium ductis describunt, esse in planis immobilibus & temporibus proportionales."

"The areas which revolving bodies describe by radii drawn to an immovable center of force lie in fixed planes and are proportional to the times."

Modern Interpretation

This proves Kepler's Second Law of planetary motion mathematically:

dA/dt = constant

Where:

  • A = area swept by radius vector
  • t = time

Implies angular momentum conservation for central forces.

Newton's Proof: Shows that under any centripetal force, equal areas are swept in equal times by demonstrating that the area rate remains constant.

Proposition XI: Elliptical Orbits

"Si corpus revolvatur in ellipsi: requiritur lex vis centripetæ tendentis ad umbilicum ellipsis."

"If a body revolves in an ellipse; it is required to find the law of the centripetal force tending to the focus of the ellipse."

Modern Interpretation

Newton proves that elliptical orbits imply an inverse-square law force:

F ∝ 1/r²

This is the first mathematical derivation of:

  • Kepler's First Law (elliptical orbits)
  • The inverse-square law of gravitation
  • Connects celestial mechanics to terrestrial physics

Corollary I

The force is inversely as the square of the distance, and therefore the periodic time is as the 3/2th power of the major axis (Kepler's Third Law).

T² ∝ a³

Section III: Motion in Resisting Media

Propositions XXI-XXXV analyze motion with resistance forces.

Proposition XXIV: Pendulum Motion

"The quantity of matter in pendulums of the same length and shape varies as the weights and the squares of the times of oscillation."

Modern Interpretation

Establishes the pendulum period formula:

T = 2π√(L/g)

Where:

  • T = period
  • L = length
  • g = gravitational acceleration

Newton used this to experimentally verify the equivalence of inertial and gravitational mass.
